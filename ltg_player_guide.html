Langelier Tactical Game

A Player's Guide · From First Turn to First Character

LTG is a small-party tactical RPG played as turn-based card combat. You build a character — stats bought with points, a spellbook translated from real Magic: the Gathering cards, and two signature moves of your own design — then fight generated enemies on a three-row battlefield. You do not need to know Magic to play; if you do, look for the MTG notes throughout.

IThe Battlefield

Two sides face each other: the party — your characters plus any allied tokens they conjure — and the enemies. Each side has three rows: front, mid, and rear. Position is abstract — rows, not a grid — but it is a live resource, not a one-time setup.

The Wall

Melee attacks can only strike the front-most occupied row of a side. A character standing in front therefore shields everyone behind them from melee, for free. Ranged and flying attacks ignore the wall and reach any row. Everything positional in LTG flows from this split: the wall answers melee for nothing, so the threats that matter are the ones the wall can't stop.

One more law worth learning early: you can never dodge a declared attack. Enemy intents lock their target when declared, and bodies physically relocate only at the end of the round. Moving is about next round — where you'll stand when the next set of intents is declared — never about slipping the current one.

IIThe Round, Turn by Turn

Combat proceeds in rounds of five steps, always in this order:

StepWhat happens
1 · UpkeepEvery character draws one card; mana refreshes; from turn 2 onward mana capacity rises by +1 (the curve-up); once-per-round abilities and enemy cooldowns reset; poison and regen counters tick; held channels fire their recurring effects.
2 · Enemy IntentsEach enemy declares what it will do this round — you see a veiled telegraph line (§VII) naming its kind of move and its target, before you act.
3 · Player ActionsEach of your characters takes their turn, in a fixed initiative order rolled once at the start of the fight.
4 · Enemy ActionsEach enemy executes its declared intent. The action goes on the stack — you can respond (§VI).
5 · End StepUntil-end-of-turn effects expire (temporary HP and pumps fade), and all pending movement resolves.

Victory when every enemy is dead or exiled. Defeat when your whole party is incapacitated. Both are checked continuously — a mid-round kill ends the fight mid-round.

IIIYour Turn: Actions

On your turn you take exactly one proactive action:

ActionWhat it does
AttackYour basic attack — damage equal to your Power — or an attack a card grants. Afterwards you may still act at instant speed, but cast no sorceries.
CastCast sorcery-speed spells — as many as your mana allows. No attack this turn.
DefendYour defensive stance: gain +3 temporary HP until end of turn.
MoveReposition to any row. Free of mana; takes effect at End Step.

Free, at any time, on anyone's turn: casting instants (limited only by mana), your once-per-turn Mitigate reaction, and your once-per-encounter Skill (§XV). None of these consume the proactive action. Two keywords bend the rules: vigilance lets you attack and cast in one turn; haste lets you take your action and make a free move.

The abilities every character owns

AbilityTimingEffect

Defendaction · 1/round+3 temporary HP until end of turn.
Mitigatereaction · 1/turnAnswer one incoming attack: reduce each of its hits by half your Power, rounded up — on yourself, or intercepting for an ally (see below).
Skillfree · 1/encounterYour authored signature trick, cast at instant speed (§XV).
Ultimateaction · 1/encounterYour authored finisher — no mana cost, but only castable on a full ultimate gauge (§XV).

Mitigate — the bodyguard rule

Mitigate's value is ceil(Power ÷ 2), read at the moment the attack resolves — a pump on you raises it. Each hit in the answered action is reduced by the full amount, independently. Used on an ally, every hit aimed at them is redirected onto you (then reduced); the ally must be in your row or an adjacent one, and intercepting moves you to their row. Covering the backline pulls the tank off the wall — every interception trades who do I save against what do I expose by leaving?

For MTG players

There is no blocking and no combat step. The basic attack is closest to a creature's swing; Mitigate is your once-a-turn "block"; and countering an enemy's attack with a card is a fully legitimate answer to it. Sorcery/instant timing works exactly as your instincts expect.

IVMana

There are no lands. Every character has a mana capacity that starts at their build's base value on turn 1 and grows by +1 every turn from turn 2 — you hit your land drop automatically, forever.

  • Each point of capacity is colour-locked when it arrives: you pick its colour from your identity ( — at most 3 distinct colours per character).
  • Locked capacity refreshes every Upkeep — spend it each round, it comes back.
  • Ramp spells raise capacity above the curve MTG: Rampant Growth — some usable immediately, some "enter tapped."
  • Rituals add mana to this turn's pool only MTG: Dark Ritual — a burst, not growth.
  • Channeled cards reserve their cost for as long as you hold them (§VIII).
  • Mana, hand, and HP all reset fresh between encounters.

VDamage & Defence

Damage is deterministic. No to-hit rolls, no dice — a 4-damage attack deals exactly 4. Your effective HP is hp + temporary HP; pumps and Defend raise the temporary half, which fades at End Step. A character at 0 HP is incapacitated — out of the fight, but restored the instant healing brings them above 0. Enemies and tokens die at 0.

Because damage always lands, defence never means dodging — it means answering the action. There are five ways:

AnswerHow
ReduceMitigate the hit, or a prevent effect MTG: Fog
AbsorbTemporary HP from Defend or the toughness half of a pump
CancelCounter the action while it's on the stack MTG: Negate
DisruptStrip or stun the enemy's intent before it ever acts
RemoveKill, exile, or bounce the attacker (bosses resist — §XIII)

VIThe Stack & Reacting

Everything that resolves goes on the stack, last-in-first-out MTG: the stack, as you know it. Before anything resolves, every character gets a chance to respond with instants or reactions — and only after the whole party passes may an eligible enemy reaction fire, which re-opens your window. You can respond to your own spells.

Every action has two properties, and both matter:

SpellAbility
Active (costs your action)sorceryattack · activated · Defend
Reactive (free)instanttriggered · Mitigate · Skill

Counters

A counter cancels one enemy action on the stack, filtered by type: an action counter stops anything; a spell counter only magic MTG: Negate; an ability counter stops attacks, activated, and triggered abilities MTG: Stifle, broadened. Since there is no blocking, countering an attack is a real answer, paid with a card. Enemies can counter too — and yes, you can counter the counter.

Targeting, hexproof, and fizzling

LTG preserves MTG's distinction exactly: an effect is targeted only if its source card says "target." "A creature you control" chooses without targeting; "target creature" targets. Hexproof blocks only enemy targeted effects — basic attacks still land, in both directions. Targeted effects re-check legality when they resolve and fizzle if the target is gone.

VIIReading the Enemy

During the Enemy Intents step, each enemy telegraphs a veiled line: the kind of move and its locked target — never the name, the numbers, or the text. You'll see lines like:

  • "The Spore Husk threatens Soren." — a hostile physical move at one hero
  • "The Myconid Mother begins casting a spell at Soren." — magic, counterable when it lands
  • "The Archer prepares an assault on the front of your party." — a row is in danger
  • "Orgen the Dungeonmaster gathers its power." — a charge is building (§XIII)
  • "The Cinderpriest turns its attention to the Bonechanter." — support; someone's getting healed

The stack is honest

Veiling is pre-stack only. The moment an enemy executes, its real action — full name, effects, amounts — appears on the stack, and your counters and instants choose against complete information. Only your pre-stack game runs on partial knowledge: who to Mitigate, who to stun, where to stand.

You can attack the intent itself, before it ever reaches the stack: strip_intent deletes it — and reveals what it would have been — MTG: targeted discard; stun makes the enemy skip it MTG: tap down; killing or debuffing the owner works too.

VIIIChanneling

Enchantments become channeled cards: sustained effects you actively hold — concentration, not a permanent on a battlefield MTG: enchantments, held not played.

  • Cost: casting one reserves its mana for as long as you hold it. Big channels lock big mana.
  • Effects are either continuous (an aura or anthem, active the whole time) or recurring (fires every Upkeep — a token engine, a damage tick).
  • Breaking: taking a single hit of ≥25% of your max HP, or being incapacitated, breaks concentration — dropping every channel you hold at once. The cards are spent and the reserved mana releases in a burst.
  • You may also drop voluntarily at instant speed — even mid-reaction, releasing the mana straight into your pool to pay for an instant.

The defensive art: the break reads the size of the hit, not the damage you take. Reduction (Mitigate, prevent) protects a channel; raw temp-HP absorption does not — the blow still lands at full size.

IXPoison, Regen & Infect

AfflictionRule
PoisonEach poison counter is a persistent −0/−1. A poison effect places its counters now, then again every Upkeep, until the victim dies, its optional turn-bound expires, or the victim receives any healing — an antidote is an antidote, even one that restores nothing. Poison is not damage: it can't be Mitigated or prevented, and it never breaks a channel. Multiple poison effects stack and tick together.
RegenThe mirror: persistent +0/+1 counters, ticking every Upkeep until the creature is dealt damage that connects. A regen tick counts as healing — regen is the over-time poison cure. Poison and regen counters annihilate 1:1.
Infect (keyword)Any damage this creature deals that connects also poisons the victim — one poison effect per landed hit, first counter arriving next Upkeep. The venomed blade wounds now, sickens later. MTG: infect, loosely — damage still hits HP

XYour Deck

Your deck — your loadout — is a 20-card singleton library: no duplicate source cards, drawn one per Upkeep, kept in hand. Rarity is a power dial with fixed quotas:

RarityQuota
Mythicexactly 1your headline card
Rareexactly 3
Uncommonexactly 6
Common10 or morethe only rarity you may exceed 20 cards with

Cards come from real MTG cards, imported by name in the Deckbuilder and translated into LTG's effect language. Only three MTG card types exist here:

  • Instants → reactive spells, castable any time you have the mana.
  • Sorceries → active spells, cast on your turn with the Cast action.
  • Enchantments → channeled cards (§VIII).

No creatures, lands, artifacts, planeswalkers, or battles. Allied creatures exist only as tokens your spells conjure; lands survive only inside ramp and ritual spells. A card's mana value is its Level, which some effects read ("destroy an enemy of Level 3 or less").

XIIIKnow Your Enemy

Enemies are asymmetric: no deck, no hand, no mana. Each has HP, a Level (its inherent power tier — shown as a Roman numeral), and a kit of telegraphed abilities. Expect support pieces that heal, control pieces that stun and taunt, escalating threats that grow every round you ignore them, and reactions that punish careless casting. You will always be outnumbered; the fight is about kill-priority.

Minions & bosses

Minions can be destroyed, exiled, or bounced. A boss is "player-class": immune to removal until its execute window — at or below 25% of max HP. Crossing that line, the boss enrages: a free, dramatic eruption you always eat, stuns and taunts shaken off, cooldowns reset, and its kit turns nastier — but from then on it can be executed. You can always damage, counter, debuff, and stun a boss; you just can't remove it early.

Charge — the gathering storm

Some enemies gather power: visible charge pips accumulate turn by turn, and at a threshold, a hidden ability detonates onto the stack — where you can still counter it. Break the windup early (kill, stun, strip) or hold an answer for the blast. Countering it still wastes the charge.

Tokens — your autonomous allies

Creatures you conjure fight alongside you, not for you: they declare their own intents, and you can help them but never command or sacrifice them. Allies die; characters are only incapacitated.

XIVBuilding a Character

A character is a 70-point build. There are no classes — the four archetypes below are just well-tuned example spends. Everyone starts from the same free baseline and buys upward:

Free baseline

8 HP · 1 mana capacity · 1 starting card · one attack mode: melee at Power 2 or ranged at Power 1. Melee hits harder for free because it's row-restricted; ranged reaches anything. You own only the mode you pick.

PurchaseCostNotes

+1 Power10 ptsmax +2 bought (melee caps at 4, ranged at 3)
One keyword (max one)5–25 ptsreach 5 · trample 10 · first strike 15 · lifelink 15 · haste 15 · vigilance 20 · flying 25

Guardrails: HP ≥ 8, mana ≥ 1, cards ≥ 1, one attack mode, at most one keyword. Protection, hexproof, indestructible, deathtouch, and infect can never be bought — they reach a hero only through spells (or, someday, gear).

Remember the hidden coupling: Mitigate = half your Power, rounded up. Buying offence buys your guard. And every character picks a colour identity (1–3 of W U B R G), which bounds both your mana colours and your deck.

XVYour Skill & Ultimate

Beyond stats and deck, every character carries two authored signature moves — designed by you, in the Deckbuilder's character editor, with the same effect language as any card. Give each a name and a line of flavour; they live on your character sheet, not in your library.

SkillUltimate
TimingInstant speed — free, any reaction window, anyone's turnAn action on your turn
UsesOnce per encounterOnce per encounter
CostOptional mana cost — your choice at design timeNever costs mana — the gauge is the cost
Gate—Castable only on a full ultimate gauge

Both resolve as abilities, not spells — an enemy counterspell cannot stop your limit break; only a broader ability-counter can.

The ultimate gauge

A public meter from 0 to 100, starting empty each encounter, filled by playing the game:

You…Gauge
take your proactive action+2
cast a spell+1 per mana spent
lose HP+1 per point
deal damage that connects+1 per point
heal or grant temporary HP+1 per point
use your Skill+5
see an ally go down+25

Note what this rewards: standing your ground charges the gauge — Defend alone is worth +5 (+2 action, +3 shield granted) — and a fallen ally surges the whole party a quarter of the way to vengeance.

Designing them well

  • Skills are answers. Instant speed and once-only means the best skills break one bad moment: a save, a reveal, a burst of mana, a perfectly timed counter.
  • Ultimates are statements. An action, gated behind ~4–6 rounds of play — it should bend the fight. Stack two or three effects; one small pump is a wasted climax.
  • Match the build. Rell's ultimate should end a duel; a Channeler's should make the ritual unbreakable for a turn, not deal 9 damage.

XVIFirst-Fight Advice

  1. Kill-priority is the whole game. Healers, escalating threats, and channelers first; the wall of meat last.
  2. Answer channels. An enemy ritual ticking every Upkeep will out-value anything you do while ignoring it. One big hit (≥25% of the channeler's max HP) breaks it.
  3. Keep hits small. The same rule protects you — reduction guards your channels; absorption doesn't.
  4. Spend your instants. Mana refreshes every round; unspent mana is a wasted turn. Reaction windows are where fights are won.
  5. Respect the veil. "Threatens" might be 2 damage or 7. When an enemy gathers its power, treat the countdown as real.
  6. Don't hoard the Skill. Once per encounter, but an encounter only lasts so long — a Skill unspent at victory answered nothing.
  7. Fall with purpose. Going down isn't dying — allies can revive you, and your fall surges every ally's gauge +25. Sometimes the tank buys the turn the ultimate needs.
