Langelier Tactical Game

A Player's Guide · From First Turn to First Character

LTG is a small-party tactical RPG played as turn-based card combat. You build a character — stats bought with points, a spellbook translated from real Magic: the Gathering cards, and two signature moves of your own design — then fight generated enemies on a three-row battlefield: one-shot encounters, or three-act adventures where your wounds and your growth carry from fight to fight. You do not need to know Magic to play; if you do, look for the MTG notes throughout.

IThe Battlefield

Two sides face each other: the party — your characters plus any allied tokens they conjure — and the enemies. Each side has three rows: front, mid, and rear. Position is abstract — rows, not a grid — but it is a live resource, not a one-time setup.

The Wall

Melee attacks can only strike the front-most occupied row of a side. A character standing in front therefore shields everyone behind them from melee, for free. Ranged and flying attacks ignore the wall and reach any row. Flyers are transparent to the wall: they neither form it nor hide behind their own bodies — ground melee runs straight beneath them to the first grounded row. Everything positional in LTG flows from this split: the wall answers melee for nothing, so the threats that matter are the ones the wall can't stop.

One law worth learning early: movement never deletes an attack — it can only change who takes it. Movement is live: your body relocates the moment a move resolves, and every pending melee intent immediately re-checks the board. Step behind a teammate and the swing meant for you redirects onto whoever now holds the line; step back with nobody covering you and it simply follows — the front-most row is wherever you're standing. Dodging is interposition: it needs a body willing to eat the hit. Ranged and flying intents never redirect at all — they stay locked on their declared target, which is exactly why Mitigate exists. And it cuts both ways: a rear-row tank can march to the front into a pending swing and take it off the caster's back.

The rows can be redrawn by force, too. Shove effects (§XII) move a body between rows the instant they resolve, and both sides have them — and because movement is live, a shove re-checks pending intents like any other move. Forcing the enemy's front-liner back mid-turn opens their wall right now, putting the backline in your melee's reach; an enemy hook that drags your caster to the front makes this round's swings and next round's intents very interested in them. Where everyone stands is a negotiation held every round.

IIThe Round, Turn by Turn

Combat proceeds in rounds of five steps, always in this order:

StepWhat happens
1 · UpkeepEvery character draws one card; mana refreshes; from turn 2 onward mana capacity rises by +1 (the curve-up); once-per-round abilities and enemy cooldowns reset; poison and regen counters tick; held channels fire their recurring effects.
2 · Enemy IntentsEach enemy declares what it will do this round — you see a veiled telegraph line (§VII) naming its kind of move and its target, before you act.
3 · Player ActionsEach of your characters takes their turn, in a fixed initiative order rolled once at the start of the fight.
4 · Enemy ActionsEach enemy executes its declared intent. The action goes on the stack — you can respond (§VI).
5 · End StepUntil-end-of-turn effects expire (temporary HP and pumps fade).

Victory when every enemy is dead or exiled. Defeat when your whole party is incapacitated. Both are checked continuously — a mid-round kill ends the fight mid-round.

IIIYour Turn: Actions

On your turn you take exactly one proactive action:

ActionWhat it does
AttackYour basic attack — damage equal to your Power — or an attack a card grants. Afterwards you may still act at instant speed, but cast no sorceries.
CastCast sorcery-speed spells — as many as your mana allows. No attack this turn.
DefendYour defensive stance: gain +3 temporary HP until end of turn.
MoveReposition to any row. Free of mana; resolves live (it goes on the stack, and pending melee intents re-check when you arrive). Cannot be countered.

Free, at any time, on anyone's turn: casting instants (limited only by mana) and your once-per-turn Mitigate reaction. Neither consumes the proactive action. Your Skill and Ultimate (§XVI) are actions — they spend the turn like Attack or Cast does. Two keywords bend the rules: vigilance lets you pair your attack with a Cast turn — or with your Skill; haste lets you take your action and make a free move.

The abilities every character owns

Ultimateaction · 1/encounterYour authored finisher — no mana cost, but only castable on a full ultimate gauge (§XVI).

One more thing can reshape this list: a held stance (§VIII) can replace — or remove — your attack, Defend, Mitigate, and Move themselves.

Mitigate — the bodyguard rule

Mitigate's value is ceil(Power ÷ 2), read at the moment the attack resolves — a pump on you raises it. Each hit in the answered action is reduced by the full amount, independently. Used on an ally, every hit aimed at them is redirected onto you (then reduced); the ally must be in your row or an adjacent one, and intercepting moves you to their row. Covering the backline pulls the tank off the wall — every interception trades who do I save against what do I expose by leaving?

For MTG players

There is no blocking and no combat step. The basic attack is closest to a creature's swing; Mitigate is your once-a-turn "block"; and countering an enemy's attack with a card is a fully legitimate answer to it. Sorcery/instant timing works exactly as your instincts expect.

IVMana

There are no lands. Every character has a mana capacity that starts at their build's base value on turn 1 and grows by +1 every turn from turn 2 — you hit your land drop automatically, forever.

  • Each point of capacity is colour-locked when it arrives: you pick its colour from your identity ( — at most 3 distinct colours per character).
  • Locked capacity refreshes every Upkeep — spend it each round, it comes back.
  • Ramp spells raise capacity above the curve MTG: Rampant Growth — some usable immediately, some "enter tapped."
  • Rituals add mana to this turn's pool only MTG: Dark Ritual — a burst, not growth.
  • Channeled cards reserve their cost for as long as you hold them (§VIII).
  • Mana, hand, and HP all reset fresh between encounters. (Adventures bend these rules — §XVII.)

VDamage & Defence

Damage is deterministic. No to-hit rolls, no dice — a 4-damage attack deals exactly 4. Your effective HP is hp + temporary HP; pumps and Defend raise the temporary half, which fades at End Step. A character at 0 HP is incapacitated — out of the fight, but restored the instant healing brings them above 0. Enemies and tokens die at 0.

Because damage always lands, defence never means dodging — it means answering the action. There are five ways:

AnswerHow
ReduceMitigate the hit, or a prevent effect MTG: Fog
AbsorbTemporary HP from Defend or the toughness half of a pump
CancelCounter the action while it's on the stack MTG: Negate
DisruptStrip or stun the enemy's intent before it ever acts
RemoveKill, exile, bounce — or dominate (§X) — the attacker (bosses resist all of it)

VIThe Stack & Reacting

Everything that resolves goes on the stack, last-in-first-out MTG: the stack, as you know it. Before anything resolves, every character gets a chance to respond with instants or reactions — and only after the whole party passes may an eligible enemy reaction fire, which re-opens your window. You can respond to your own spells. (The game passes for you automatically whenever you have nothing playable — you'll only ever be asked when a real choice exists.)

Every action has two properties, and both matter:

SpellAbility
Active (costs your action)sorceryattack · activated · Defend · Skill · Ultimate
Reactive (free)instanttriggered · Mitigate

Counters

A counter cancels one enemy action on the stack, filtered by type: an action counter stops anything; a spell counter only magic MTG: Negate; an ability counter stops attacks, activated, and triggered abilities MTG: Stifle, broadened. Since there is no blocking, countering an attack is a real answer, paid with a card. Enemies can counter too — and yes, you can counter the counter.

Targeting, hexproof, and fizzling

LTG preserves MTG's distinction exactly: an effect is targeted only if its source card says "target." "A creature you control" chooses without targeting; "target creature" targets. Hexproof blocks only enemy targeted effects — basic attacks still land, in both directions. Targeted effects re-check legality when they resolve and fizzle if the target is gone.

VIIReading the Enemy

During the Enemy Intents step, each enemy telegraphs a veiled line: the kind of move and its current target — never the name, the numbers, or the text. (A melee target can still change mid-round: live movement redirects a swing onto whoever interposes, and the line updates the moment it happens — unless the enemy is relentless, whose intents pursue their declared target anywhere.) You'll see lines like:

  • "The Spore Husk threatens Soren." — a hostile physical move at one hero
  • "The Myconid Mother begins casting a spell at Soren." — magic, counterable when it lands
  • "The Archer prepares an assault on the front of your party." — a row is in danger
  • "Orgen the Dungeonmaster gathers its power." — a charge is building (§XIV)
  • "The Cinderpriest turns its attention to the Bonechanter." — support; someone's getting healed

The stack is honest

Veiling is pre-stack only. The moment an enemy executes, its real action — full name, effects, amounts — appears on the stack, and your counters and instants choose against complete information. Only your pre-stack game runs on partial knowledge: who to Mitigate, who to stun, where to stand.

You can attack the intent itself, before it ever reaches the stack: strip_intent deletes it — and reveals what it would have been — MTG: targeted discard; stun makes the enemy skip it MTG: tap down; killing or debuffing the owner works too. One enemy deserves special mention: an enraged boss telegraphs two lines a round (§XIV) — a strip removes whichever one you choose, a stun silences one but never both.

VIIIChanneling & Stances

Enchantments become channeled cards: sustained effects a character actively holds — concentration, not a permanent on a battlefield MTG: enchantments, held not played.

  • Cost: casting one reserves its mana for as long as you hold it. Big channels lock big mana.
  • Effects are either continuous (an aura or anthem, active the whole time) or recurring (fires every Upkeep — a token engine, a damage tick).
  • Breaking: taking a single hit of ≥25% of your max HP, or being incapacitated, breaks concentration — dropping every channel you hold at once. The cards are spent and the reserved mana releases in a burst.
  • You may also drop voluntarily at instant speed — even mid-reaction, releasing the mana straight into your pool to pay for an instant.

The defensive art: the break reads the size of the hit, not the damage you take. Reduction (Mitigate, prevent) protects a channel; raw temp-HP absorption does not — the blow still lands at full size.

Stances — fighting in a form

A stance is a special channeled effect — a card from your library, or a channeled Skill (§XVI) — that rewires your body while you hold it. For each of your four main abilities — attack, Defend, Mitigate, Move — a stance declares one of three things: unchanged, removed (you simply cannot do that while in this form), or replaced with a new authored action.

  • Replacements keep their slot. A replaced attack is still your once-a-round action; a replaced Mitigate is still your once-a-turn reaction to an incoming attack — its new effects resolve instead of the reduction.
  • Removal is total. No Move also means no haste free move; no attack also means no first-strike held attack; no Mitigate means you guard nobody, yourself included.
  • Your spellbook is untouchable. Casting, instants, your Skill, and your Ultimate are never stance-modified. A stance rewires your body, not your magic.
  • One stance at a time, and it breaks like any channel — one hit of ≥25% of your max HP knocks you out of the form, along with everything else you were holding.

Two classic shapes: Meditative Trance — attack removed, Defend becomes "heal an ally 3": the healer stops fighting and starts mending, until someone breaks the trance. Stoneskin — Move removed, Defend becomes "indestructible until end of turn": rooted, immovable, nearly unkillable — and the counterplay is written on it.

IXPoison, Regen & Infect

AfflictionRule
PoisonEach poison counter is a persistent −0/−1. A poison effect places its counters now, then again every Upkeep, until the victim dies, its optional turn-bound expires, or the victim receives any healing — and healing is a true antidote: it ends the ticking and sheds every accumulated poison counter, even if the heal restores nothing. Poison is not damage: it can't be Mitigated or prevented, and it never breaks a channel. Multiple poison effects stack and tick together.
RegenThe mirror: persistent +0/+1 counters, ticking every Upkeep until the creature is dealt damage that connects. A regen tick counts as healing — it stops poison's ticking (though only a direct heal purges the counters; regen instead cancels them out one-for-one as its own counters land).
Infect (keyword)Any damage this creature deals that connects also poisons the victim — one poison effect per landed hit, first counter arriving next Upkeep. The venomed blade wounds now, sickens later. MTG: infect, loosely — damage still hits HP

XThe Dead & the Dominated

The dead stay on the battlefield. When an enemy dies — killed by damage, poison, or a destroy effect — it leaves a corpse on the row where it fell: a dim marker, an object rather than a creature. You can't attack, damage, or heal a corpse; it just lies there. Until someone does something with it.

Destroy vs exile — at last, a difference

VerbWhat's left
destroy / deathA corpse. Death triggers fire. Necromancy can find it.
exileNothing. No corpse, no death trigger, nothing to raise. Exile also works on a corpse — burn the body, deny the necromancer.

Tokens never leave corpses (raised undead cannot be re-raised — no treadmills), and your characters never die at all — they're incapacitated, not corpses.

Control — one spell, two dark arts

The control effect makes an enemy fight for you, for a duration (some turns, or the whole encounter). Its flavour depends on what you aim it at:

  • On a living enemy — mind control MTG: Act of Treason · Mind Control. It crosses to your side with its current HP, Power, and keywords, but loses all its clever tricks — a dominated mind is a blunt instrument.
  • On a corpse — raise dead MTG: Raise Dead · Animate Dead. The corpse is consumed and an undead servant rises where it lay: the dead creature's Power, half its HP, on your side. When the spell ends, it crumbles.

Controlled creatures run the simplest brain in the game: every round, basic attack against the closest enemy. They're autonomous allies — help them, never command them. And two iron rules: bosses can never be controlled — not alive, not in their execute window, not as corpses; and control never wins the fight — if a controlled enemy would ever be the last one standing, the spell breaks on the spot and it snaps back to its own side. Like bounce, domination buys actions, never the win.

The stirring dead

Some enemies rise: when one dies, its corpse visibly stirs — and a few Upkeeps later it stands back up at half HP, once per fight. A stirring corpse means the enemy is not yet defeated, and the fight will not end while it stirs. Your answers: exile the body, or raise it yourself — a stolen corpse rises on your side instead.

Enemy necromancers play this whole section against you: they raise their own fallen as fresh undead, turning every corpse on their side into a threat. Kill-priority incarnate — and the reason exile stops being a luxury against the deathless.

XIYour Deck

Your deck — your loadout — is a 20-card singleton library: no duplicate source cards, drawn one per Upkeep, kept in hand. Rarity is a power dial with fixed quotas:

RarityQuota
Mythicexactly 1your headline card
Rareexactly 3
Uncommonexactly 6
Common10 or morethe only rarity you may exceed 20 cards with

Cards come from real MTG cards, imported by name in the Deckbuilder and translated into LTG's effect language. Only three MTG card types exist here:

  • Instants → reactive spells, castable any time you have the mana.
  • Sorceries → active spells, cast on your turn with the Cast action.
  • Enchantments → channeled cards, including stances (§VIII).

No creatures, lands, artifacts, planeswalkers, or battles. Allied creatures exist only as tokens your spells conjure (or steal — §X); lands survive only inside ramp and ritual spells. A card's mana value is its Level, which some effects read ("destroy an enemy of Level 3 or less").

Area shapes — rows and blasts

Damaging effects come in footprints. A row effect hits everyone on a named row. A splash effect picks one creature and also catches its whole row; a blast catches its row and all adjacent rows. Adjacency: front↔mid and mid↔rear — front and rear are not adjacent, so a blast centred on the mid row catches everything on that side. The centre of the formation is the splash magnet; standing there is a choice. Only the picked creature is targeted — hexproof shields a creature from being the pick, never from being caught in the spray.

Cards may also be modal ("choose one…") or carry conditional riders ("if cast as a reaction…", "if the target is dead…") — both work the way an MTG charm or kicker leads you to expect.

XIVKnow Your Enemy

Enemies are asymmetric: no deck, no hand, no mana. Each has HP, a Level (its inherent power tier — shown as a Roman numeral), and a kit of telegraphed abilities. Expect support pieces that heal, control pieces that stun and taunt, escalating threats that grow every round you ignore them, hooks that drag your backline into the open, necromancers that raise their fallen (§X), war-chanters that prime a double-strength blow (§XII), and reactions that punish careless casting — including mirrors that copy your own spell and send it back at its caster. You will always be outnumbered; the fight is about kill-priority.

Minions & bosses

Minions can be destroyed, exiled, bounced — or dominated. A boss is "player-class": immune to removal and to control, until its execute window — at or below 25% of max HP. Crossing that line, the boss enrages: a free, dramatic eruption you always eat, stuns and taunts shaken off, cooldowns reset — and from then on it can be executed. You can always damage, counter, debuff, stun, and even shove a boss (moving it is fair play — it affects the boss in place); you just can't remove it early.

Boss fury — the endgame bites back

An enraged boss declares and executes two intents every round, each veiled and targeted separately — two lines in the intents window, two threats to answer with one Mitigate. A stun silences one of the two, never both; a strip removes whichever you choose. The final act of a boss fight is a race you must run forward: the execute window is open, but so is the throttle.

Charge — the gathering storm

Some enemies gather power: visible charge pips accumulate turn by turn, and at a threshold, a hidden ability detonates onto the stack — where you can still counter it. Break the windup early (kill, stun, strip) or hold an answer for the blast. Countering it still wastes the charge.

Tokens — your autonomous allies

Creatures you conjure — or raise, or dominate — fight alongside you, not for you: they declare their own intents, and you can help them but never command or sacrifice them. Allies die; characters are only incapacitated.

XVBuilding a Character

A character is a 70-point build. There are no classes — the four archetypes below are just well-tuned example spends. Everyone starts from the same free baseline and buys upward:

Free baseline

8 HP · 1 mana capacity · 1 starting card · one attack mode: melee at Power 2 or ranged at Power 1. Melee hits harder for free because it's row-restricted; ranged reaches anything. You own only the mode you pick.

Guardrails: HP ≥ 8, mana ≥ 1, cards ≥ 1, one attack mode, at most one keyword — for the character's whole life, level-ups included. Protection, hexproof, indestructible, deathtouch, and infect can never be bought — they reach a hero only through spells (or, someday, gear).

XVIYour Skill & Ultimate

Beyond stats and deck, every character carries two authored signature moves — designed by you, in the Deckbuilder's character editor, with the same effect language as any card. Give each a name and a line of flavour; they live on your character sheet, not in your library.

The ultimate gauge

A public meter from 0 to 100, starting empty each encounter, filled by playing the game:

You…Gauge
take your proactive action+2
cast a spell+1 per mana spent
lose HP+1 per point
deal damage that connects+1 per point
heal or grant temporary HP+1 per point
use your Skill+5
see an ally go down+25

Note what this rewards: standing your ground charges the gauge — Defend alone is worth +5 (+2 action, +3 shield granted) — and a fallen ally surges the whole party a quarter of the way to vengeance. In an adventure, half your gauge carries between acts (§XVII): finish Act I full, open Act II at 50.

Designing them well

  • Skills are signatures. A skill spends your turn, so make the turn worth it: a tempo swing (strip an intent, see the top of your deck), a combo primer (your next spell damage doubled), or a channeled form — even a personal stance — that no card in your library could hold for you.
  • Ultimates are statements. An action, gated behind ~4–6 rounds of play — it should bend the fight. Stack two or three effects; one small pump is a wasted climax.
  • Match the build. Rell's ultimate should end a duel; a Channeler's should make the ritual unbreakable for a turn, not deal 9 damage.

XVIIAdventures

An encounter is one fight. An adventure is a story: three acts against one theme, fought back to back by the same party — guards at the gate, knights in the courtyard, the tyrant in his throne room. Each act opens with a line of narrative and its own painted scene; each act is harder than the last; Act III always ends in the boss, and the earlier acts may hide a mini-boss (a full boss in every mechanical sense — enrage, fury, and all — just a lesser one: the gate-captain, not the king).

Starting one is the same New Game screen: pick your party on the left, then choose either an Encounter or an Adventure. Win Act III and the adventure is yours. Fall in any act and the run is over — no checkpoints, no retries mid-run; the adventure stays on the list, and the door is always open to march back in from Act I.

Levelling up

Between acts, every character levels up: the victory splash, then the level-up screen — your portrait beside the same points-buy panel you built the character with — then the next act's narrative, then the fight.

  • +30 points per level, on the same price list as creation.
  • Bank freely: spend 25 now and you'll have 35 next time. Confirming is mandatory; spending is not.
  • No take-backs: everything bought at creation or a previous level is locked.
  • Colours never change. New mana capacity picks its colour within your existing identity.
  • One keyword, ever — though a character who took None at creation may finally buy theirs here.
  • The Power ceiling rises with you: total bought Power ≤ 2 × your level (+2 at creation, +4 at level 2, +6 at level 3).
  • HP purchases heal: +2 max HP is also +2 current HP — the only healing money can buy mid-run, at roughly 2½ points per HP.
  • It's all temporary: the levelled build lives and dies with this adventure. Your saved character is untouched.

The enemy is levelling too — each act is budgeted a full tier above the last, and you carry your wounds while they arrive fresh. Expect Act III to ask for everything the first two acts taught you.

XVIIIFirst-Fight Advice

  1. Kill-priority is the whole game. Healers, necromancers, escalating threats, and channelers first; the wall of meat last.
  2. Answer channels. An enemy ritual ticking every Upkeep will out-value anything you do while ignoring it. One big hit (≥25% of the channeler's max HP) breaks it.
  3. Keep hits small. The same rule protects you — reduction guards your channels; absorption doesn't.
  4. Spend your instants. Mana refreshes every round; unspent mana is a wasted turn. Reaction windows are where fights are won.
  5. Respect the veil. "Threatens" might be 2 damage or 7. When an enemy gathers its power, treat the countdown as real.
  6. Stand where the blast isn't. The mid row is adjacent to everything — the centre of the formation is the splash magnet. Spread out when the fireballs start; just remember what the wall was holding.
  7. Tend the dead. A stirring corpse is a fight that isn't over; a necromancer beside a corpse is a fight getting longer. Exile the body — or raise it yourself and send it back the way it came.
  8. Don't hoard the Skill. Once per encounter, but an encounter only lasts so long — a Skill unspent at victory was a signature never signed. It costs a turn now, so pick the turn it wins.
  9. Fall with purpose. Going down isn't dying — allies can revive you, and your fall surges every ally's gauge +25. Sometimes the tank buys the turn the ultimate needs.
  10. In an adventure, every HP is the run's HP. A fight won at death's door is a debt Act III collects. Win cleanly — and remember the level-up screen sells the only healing between acts.
